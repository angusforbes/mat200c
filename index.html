Description
Practitioners and researchers of the media arts, as members of an evolving, interdisciplinary community, must be prepared to engage with multiple disciplines and to promote their work to a variety of audiences. In order to make an impact it is necessary to have a clear awareness of who these audiences are, what their primary agendas consist of, and what their expectations are regarding what constitutes valid research. Academic disciplines and avenues of inquiry are continually evolving or being created, and the media artist/technologist must also be able to quickly figure out the relevant aspects of these emerging fields. Moreover, interdisciplinarity is itself an ongoing research topic involving various collaborations and mediations. To these ends, this course explores a wide variety of methods that aim to faciliate the transition of students into researchers within the media arts community and academic communities at large. Each student will investigate the conventions relevant to their interests, as well as the conventions related to media arts and technology as a discipline in and of itself. Through a variety of exercises, such as analyzing seminal and current articles, researching conference proceedings, giving practice talks, and copious amounts of writing, students will prepare to have the skills to further define and defend their research.

In addition to promoting writing and presentation skills, the course has a secondary goal of engaging students in the discourse of evaluation. What is worthwhile research? What strategies are appropriate, original, and compelling? What metrics do you use to evaluate research? How can you best justify your own projects, both their aesthetic and their implementation? The class will discuss metrics for these question, and also decide when and in what contexts these questions, or other questions, are worth asking. The aim is to provide a background where students think of their work in the context of particular line or lines of artistic or engineering research. That is, to focus on the evaluation of the conceptual importance rather than merely the more narrow implementation details.

Grading will be almost entirely based on in-class participation, short writing and creative assignments, and the submission of a peer-reviewed research paper submission to a prestigious conference or journal. This submission does not have to be based on new work; rather students are encouraged to take existing projects developed previously or concurrently to this course and find appropriate venues in which to present this work. All students are required to post weekly writing assignments to a public blog using the framework of their choice (e.g. WordPress, Tumblr, Posterous).

Week 0: Course Overview
Topics: Self-presentation, artist statement, research statements, biography, curriculum vitae.
Homework (due Thursday, 3.31):
1. Set-up a research blog (if you don't already have one), and make sure comments are enabled. Don't worry too much about the way it looks at this point, you can tweak it later on.
2. Post an entry listing 3 artists and 3 researchers who you identify with and describe what you admire about their work and in what ways you might have similar research agendas.
3. Post an entry that evaluates interesting aspects of their artist/research statements and/or biographies. How do you evaluate them? Why do you think they presented themselves in the way that they did? What audiences are they aiming their materials at?
Homework (due Tuesday, 4.5):
1. Post an entry listing 20 different ideas that you might plausibly write a paper about over the next 9 weeks. Include a good title (i.e., clear, inviting, interesting) and an indicator of what specific fields each of the papers belong to. If you get stuck, use our library as a resource. You can search through the contents of thousands of electronic journals via the library website.
2. Choose 3 of these paper ideas. Find peer-reviewed journals that would be appropriate for them. Write an abstract for each of these 3 ideas using the style of one of the journals. Post them as an entry.
3. Comment on each other's abstracts (at least one abstract from each of your peers) using some of the simple evaluation strategies we discussed in class. Focus on the concept more than the writing stlye.
